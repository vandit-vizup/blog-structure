Top Navigation
The nav appears on every page — it's free real estate for brand recall, internal linking, and conversion. Most blogs let this slot go to waste with a generic site nav.

What to include
  • Logo linking to homepage (brand anchor text signal)
  • Key product pages in dropdown menus
  • Visible "Blog" link for crawlability
  • Primary CTA button (Get Started / Book Demo)
  • Secondary CTA (Login / Watch Demo)
Why it matters

Sitewide nav links pass PageRank to your most important pages. A clear CTA converts readers who arrive organically — don't let them bounce without a next step.

02
Breadcrumb + Social Share
Breadcrumbs tell Google exactly where this article sits in your site architecture. Social share buttons reduce friction for readers who want to amplify your content.
SEO Distribution
Wireframe — Breadcrumb + Share
Home / Blog / Category / Article title…
Share:
What to include
  • Home → Category → Subcategory → Article trail
  • BreadcrumbList structured data (JSON-LD)
  • LinkedIn, X, and email share buttons
  • Optional: "Copy link" icon for quick sharing
Why it matters

Breadcrumbs enable sitelinks in SERPs, reinforce your internal linking structure, and help Google understand topic clusters. Structured data = richer search appearance.

03
Author + Trust Markers
Google's E-E-A-T framework rewards content from demonstrably expert authors. Visible credentials, publication date, and review badges signal both to search engines and to human readers that your content is trustworthy.

What to include
  • Author avatar, name, and job title
  • Years of experience or relevant credential
  • "Reviewed by [Expert]" badge if applicable
  • Last updated date (freshness signal to Google)
  • Estimated read time
Why it matters

YMYL and competitive topics get extra E-E-A-T scrutiny. Author markup in Person schema lets Google associate expertise with your domain. Credibility seen above the fold reduces bounce rate immediately.

04
Article Heading + Publish Meta
The H1 is the single highest-impact on-page SEO element. It sets the expectation for the entire article. Category tags help readers self-select and strengthen topical clusters.

What to include
  • H1 with primary keyword (natural placement, not stuffed)
  • Topic category tag linking to category archive
  • Original publish date + last updated date
  • Estimated reading time
  • Author name (clickable to author page)
Why it matters

One H1 per page is non-negotiable. Category tags build crawl paths and topic cluster architecture. Publish date + "Updated" signals freshness to Google and reduces user bounce driven by content staleness.

05
Featured Image
The featured image is your Open Graph image, your thumbnail in search results, and the visual hook that keeps readers engaged. It also directly impacts Core Web Vitals if not optimised.
Core Web Vitals Brand recall
Wireframe — Featured Image
Featured Image
16:9 ratio · 1200×630px minimum
Alt text: "[primary keyword] — [brand name]"
OG Image
What to include
  • 1200×630px minimum (OG image compatible)
  • Descriptive alt text with primary keyword
  • WebP format + explicit width/height attributes
  • Brand logo or visual consistency across blog
  • loading="eager" on featured image (above the fold)
Why it matters

LCP (Largest Contentful Paint) is often triggered by the featured image. Unoptimised images drag down your Core Web Vitals score. Alt text is a direct ranking signal and accessibility requirement.

What to include
  • Left TOC with anchor links (sticky on desktop)
  • H2/H3 heading hierarchy throughout article
  • Callout blocks for key stats/quotes
  • Inline images/tables with descriptive alt text

07
Full-Width CTA Block
A reader who reaches the end of an article has demonstrated high intent. This is the highest-value conversion slot on the entire page — yet most blogs don't have one at all.
Conversion
Wireframe — Full-Width CTA
Headline + supporting copy + social proof
Primary CTA
Secondary CTA
What to include
  • Headline tied to the article topic (not generic)
  • 1-line value proposition or social proof
  • Primary CTA button (trial, demo, signup)
  • Secondary CTA (less commitment — "watch demo" or "read more")
  • Optional: number of customers or a recognisable logo strip
Why it matters

End-of-article readers have the lowest bounce intent on your site. A congruent CTA — matched to the article topic — converts at 2–4x the rate of a generic one. This is the most missed conversion slot in B2B content.

08
Author Bio Card
The post-article author bio is a critical E-E-A-T signal. It establishes the human expert behind the content, links to their other work, and builds trust at the moment readers are deciding whether to act.
E-E-A-T Authority
Wireframe — Author Bio Card
Article by
Author Name
LinkedIn · More articles →
What to include
  • Author photo, name, and role
  • 2–3 sentence bio with specific credentials
  • Link to author archive page (for SEO and topical authority)
  • LinkedIn or social profile link
  • Person schema markup on the author page
Why it matters

Google's Quality Rater Guidelines specifically call out "Who is responsible for this content?" A named, credentialed, linkable author is a direct E-E-A-T signal. Anonymous blogs lose ranking ground to attributed ones in competitive topics.

Related Blog Posts
A related posts section extends session time, reduces bounce rate, and — most importantly for SEO — creates internal links that reinforce your topic cluster structure across every article.

What to include
  • 3 topically related articles (same cluster, not random)
  • Article thumbnail, headline, and author for each card
  • "View all articles" link to the blog index
  • Keyword-rich anchor text in the card titles
  • Tag or category label on each card
Why it matters

Topically clustered internal links pass PageRank within your subject area and signal to Google that your site is authoritative on that topic. Random "popular posts" plugins kill this — related posts must match the article's topic.

Footer
The footer is sitewide and appears on every page. It's your last chance to send authority to your most important pages, surface trust signals, and meet legal requirements that Google checks.

What to include
  • Logo + one-line company description
  • Links to key product/service pages
  • Privacy Policy, Terms, Cookie Policy (legal + crawlability)
  • Social profile links (brand entity signals)
  • Trust badges: G2/Capterra ratings, SOC2, GDPR
  • Sitemap link for crawler discovery
Why it matters

Footer links pass authority sitewide. Privacy/legal pages are crawled by Google to assess site legitimacy. Social profile links help Google resolve your brand entity in the Knowledge Graph. Missing these is a silent ranking penalty.
